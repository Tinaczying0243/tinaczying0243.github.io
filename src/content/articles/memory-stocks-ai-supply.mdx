The Global Memory Index, a benchmark tracking the world's seven largest memory and storage companies, including South Korea's SK Hynix Inc., Samsung Electronics Co., and Boise, Idaho-based Micron Technology Inc., surged about 440% to an all-time high since last September, as artificial-intelligence companies race to lock down supply.

Tech giants such as Meta Platforms Inc. and Amazon.com Inc. are projected to invest about $630 billion on AI chips and data centers alone, Morgan Stanley estimates, fueling demand for semiconductor chipmakers. Projects like OpenAI's Stargate are reportedly locking up 40% of memory chip supply from Samsung and SK Hynix, which account for 65% of the global memory supply.

The AI buildout is delivering record profits to chipmakers and triggering memory shortages, sending chip prices up about 60% last quarter with further gains expected. The squeeze drives memory chipmakers to steer supply toward higher-margin customers like AI data centers rather than consumer electronics.

"Right now, it's memory," said Brad Lightcap, OpenAI's Chief Operating Officer. Memory shortages and energy constraints are limiting the expansion of AI infrastructure. Other tech industry leaders including Elon Musk and Tim Cook, the chief executive officers of Tesla Inc. and Apple Inc., are also talking about a global memory shortage, Bloomberg reported.

The spot price of mainstream 16-gigabit Double Data Rate 5 (DDR5) memory chips used in most new personal computers and entry-level servers surged sixfold during the past eight months, to $32.25 per chip from $5.13, according to pricing tracker InSpectrum. The spot price for the mainstream NAND flash chips used in most smartphones and solid-state drives has also skyrocketed about 635% to $21.93 from $2.98 per chip since last September.

The rally propelled chipmakers to record earnings. SK Hynix shares increased nearly sevenfold over the past year to $896 from $125, while Micron shares followed a similar trajectory, to $524 from $76. Micron's last-quarter revenue jumped 196% to $23.86 billion.

SK Hynix reported first-quarter operating profit of $25.4 billion, beating the $23.7 billion analyst consensus, on revenue of $35.4 billion, almost triple the year-earlier figure, Chief Financial Officer Woo-Hyun Kim said.

Samsung Electronics Co. plans to invest more than $73.3 billion in chip capacity and research in 2026, a 22% increase, as it seeks to reclaim the AI memory lead from SK Hynix Inc. Micron Technology Inc. is following close behind, with capital spending set to top $25 billion this fiscal year, above the $22.4 billion analyst consensus.

Samsung, SK Hynix and Micron, which together control almost 90% of the global market for high-bandwidth memory (HBM) chips, are reshaping their factory floors around the technology. Every Nvidia AI accelerator carries six to eight stacks of HBM, a specialized stacked dynamic random access memory (DRAM) chip that feeds AI processors at ultra-high speeds. HBM is made from the same wafers, the polished silicon discs from which hundreds of memory chips are cut, that go into ordinary PC memory.

As the three suppliers shift their best wafers to HBM, where margins run several times higher, supply for everyday DDR5 dries up. AI servers compound the squeeze, devouring terabytes of conventional memory and racks of flash storage on top. One source of demand, three product lines under strain.

In October 2025, OpenAI struck a deal with Samsung and SK Hynix to supply chips for its Stargate AI infrastructure project. The arrangement is expected to require about 900,000 DRAM wafers a month, accounting for roughly 40% of global DRAM output, Samsung said.

Meta Platforms Inc. is targeting capital expenditure of $115 billion to $135 billion in 2026, nearly double last year's $72 billion. The bulk will go to AI computing infrastructure and to recruiting researchers for "superintelligent" AI. Hyperscalers will direct about 30% of their capital spending toward memory chips in 2026, up from roughly 8% in 2023 and 2024, SemiAnalysis estimates.

The memory shortage is taking a significant toll on consumer electronics, driving up prices for everyday memory sticks, gaming consoles and smartphones. ESPO ETF, an exchange-traded fund tracking the global gaming industry, declined about 23% since October, falling from a peak of around $122 per share to roughly $94, according to data compiled by Bloomberg. Major gaming companies including Sony Group Corp. and Nintendo Co. have announced product delays or price increases as memory costs ripple through the supply chain.

Global smartphone shipments are forecast to fall 12% to below 1.1 billion units in 2026, the lowest since 2013, according to Counterpoint Research. International Data Corp. (IDC) estimates the memory crunch will permanently shrink the industry's addressable market, with shipments unlikely to return to 2025 levels even after prices stabilize around mid-2027. "It's almost permanently uneconomical to make those sub-$150 devices," said Nabila Popal, IDC's senior research director.

"Customers are prioritizing procurement over price," said Kim. Although suppliers are resuming fab construction, completing meaningful new capacity will take time, the company said in the latest earnings call in April, adding the "favorable pricing environment" will continue.

"A new equilibrium may not be reached for another two to three years, when memory prices are expected to stabilize," said Qin Gao, the principal engineer at Western Digital, a leading manufacturer of data storage solutions. He said constraints might ease once new fabs from Micron and SK Hynix come online, though these facilities are not expected to be completed until as late as the end of 2027.

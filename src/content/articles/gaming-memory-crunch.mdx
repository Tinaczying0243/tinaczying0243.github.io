For years, building a PC was the gamer's cheapest path to a better rig. That may no longer be the case. Memory chip makers are steering supply toward higher-margin customers like AI data centers, sending memory prices up about 60% last quarter, with further gains expected. The squeeze is rippling across industries, but it's taking a significant, big toll on the $200 billion gaming industry: enthusiasts can no longer afford to build or upgrade their own PCs, and the console and hardware prices are climbing.

"I've been trying to upgrade my 3-year-old PC with new components, and it's just frustrating to have nowhere to go," said Raunak Oberoi, a game enthusiast and a product manager who typically runs about 100 tabs at once. Every time he plays Dota 2 or Deadlock with Discords on, or takes a Teams call when the 3D model is active, he has to close a few apps to avoid severe lagging.

The solution is simple: add more memory. The only reason that stops him is the prices. Oberoi purchased a pair of 32GB Corsair memory sticks for $250 in 2024; the exact same product would cost him around $1200 on the manufacturer's website. Prices also surged on the secondary market, including Facebook Marketplace, due to low availability and high consumer demand. "It's just frustrating that regular consumers like me are priced out by the big tech companies," Oberoi said.

It's a race against time. Some gamers managed to beat the clock. Reddit user a_rogue_planet, a wildlife photographer in Ohio who has been gaming since 1985, upgraded his PC just as rumors of AI companies stockpiling memory began to emerge. "I got lucky with timing. I bought 32GB for my machine at about $90 in August or so, and then another 64GB in late October just as rumors of AI were beginning to emerge," said the Reddit user, who asked to be identified only by his handle because his real name is distinctive enough to make him easily searchable online. "The 64GB had moved up in price, about $30 to $210, so I felt there was something to the rumors."

His friends told him to wait until the holidays for better deals. "By then it had gotten vastly more expensive, and there were no good deals," he said. The same 64GB kit that cost him $210 in October would run several times that today.

The spot price of mainstream 16-gigabit DDR5 memory chips used in most new PCs and entry-level servers has surged roughly sixfold over the past eight months, from $5.13 to $32.25 per chip, according to pricing tracker InSpectrum. The spot price for the mainstream NAND flash chips used in most smartphones and solid-state drives has also skyrocketed about 635% from $2.98 to $21.93 each since last September.

The memory industry is dominated by three players: South Korea's Samsung Electronics Co. and SK Hynix Inc., along with Boise, Idaho-based Micron Technology Inc. Together, they control almost 90% of the global market for high-bandwidth memory (HBM), a specialized stacked-DRAM chip that feeds AI processors with data at ultra-high speeds. In October 2025, OpenAI struck a deal with Samsung and SK Hynix to supply chips for its Stargate AI infrastructure project, an arrangement expected to require about 900,000 DRAM wafers, the polished silicon discs from which hundreds of memory chips are cut, each month, which takes up roughly 40% of global DRAM output, Samsung said.

The fallout has also battered gaming companies since September. ESPO ETF, an exchange-traded fund tracking the global gaming industry, has declined from recent highs, reflecting a broader slowdown in the global gaming sector. The stock has slid about 23% since October, falling from a peak of around $122 per share to roughly $94, according to data compiled by Bloomberg.

Several major gaming companies have announced product delays or price adjustments due to the surges of memory prices, which affects gaming hardware. Valve's Steam Deck is facing stock shortages amid a memory crunch, while its Steam Machine has also been delayed. Nintendo is weighing a price increase for the Switch 2 as well.

Best Buy now lists Lenovo's handheld at $1,499 with a Ryzen Z2 and $1,999 with a Z2 Extreme. The higher-end model previously retailed for $1,349, marking a $650 increase in just six months, according to the Verge.

Sony Group Corp. announced a global price increase for its PlayStation 5 consoles and accessories on March 27, citing "continued pressures in the global economic landscape." The hikes take effect April 2 and vary slightly by region and model but average about 20%. In the US, the PS5 Digital Edition will rise $100 to $599.99 on April 2, while the standard PS5 climbs $100 to $649.99 and the flagship PS5 Pro jumps $150 to $899.99. The PlayStation Portal Remote Player will rise to $249.99.

The fallout is starting to show up in hardware sales. PlayStation 5 unit sales have slipped around 6% to around 2.3 million in April, from about 2.5 million units in November last year. While the hardware sales have witnessed a moderate decrease in the latter half of the console cycle, "Software revenue from the PlayStation Store reached a record high during the quarter," said Lin Tao, the Chief Financial Officer of Sony, on the company's most recent earnings call. The growth is primarily driven by the contribution of major third-party franchise titles and new hit releases.

"We intend to minimize the impact of the increased memory cost on this segment going forward by prioritizing monetization of the installed base to date and striving to further expand our software and network service revenue," Lin Tao said.

The bigger question for every gamer, publisher, and console maker holding their breath is how long the squeeze will last. The answer, from people who have watched chip factories up close, isn't encouraging.

"A new equilibrium may not be reached for another two to three years, when memory prices are expected to stabilize," said Qin Gao, the principal engineer at Western Digital, a leading manufacturer of data storage solutions. According to him, constraints could ease once new fabs from Micron Technology and SK Hynix come online, though these facilities are not expected to be completed until as late as the end of 2027.

"I know it takes years to build a fab. I know it takes over 2 years to build a data center because I watched one being built near me," Reddit user a_rogue_planet said. For him, the crunch is about more than chip prices. He told his 22-year-old daughter about the fear and dread for the world when she's my age. "My life at her age is starting to sound like some mythical utopia to her. You could rely on some really basic things about how the world worked."

"Who exactly wants to live in the world where you buy intelligence on a meter like electricity or water, as Sam Altman openly claims to be his end goal?" a_rogue_planet said. "This sounds like true hell to me, and many others too."
